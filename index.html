Abstract: GravityBench is an environment-based benchmark designed to evaluate the abilities of AI agents to autonomously discover gravitational physics. It challenges agents to engage in a full cycle of scientific discovery, including hypothesis formation, experimental planning under limited resources, data analysis, and evidence-driven reasoning. GravityBench features physics simulations that deviate from real-world gravitational laws, allowing robust evaluation of agent generalization. Baseline experiments indicate current leading AI models struggle significantly with planning strategically under uncertainty. GravityBench aims to provide clear, rigorous insight into agents' capabilities and limitations in scientific reasoning and discovery.

Recent AI advances have substantially improved performance in language, math, and even scientific literature analysis. However, genuine "scientific reasoning", involving iterative hypothesis testing, active experimentation, and systematic data-driven inference under meaningful uncertainty, remains poorly understood. Understanding agents' capabilities for scientific discovery is practically crucial‚Äîit could enable autonomous scientific progress or highlight serious limitations of existing architectures.

This paper introduces GravityBench, a controlled environment benchmark rigorously designed to evaluate the capability of AI agents in discovering gravitational physics. GravityBench challenges agents to actively reason about observations, plan strategically under resource constraints, and generalize beyond familiar textbook knowledge.

We believe current limitations in these areas reflect profound gaps in AI agents' understanding of the scientific discovery process itself. To help the community characterize and ultimately bridge these gaps, we contribute GravityBench as a carefully designed, transparent, and fully open-source platform.

Novel Contributions

  • Rigorous Simulated Environment: GravityBench uses high-precision gravitational simulations designed specifically to evaluate scientific reasoning and discovery.
  • Comprehensive Evaluation of Scientific Reasoning: Extends evaluation beyond standard question-answering to include experimental design, iterative data collection, and dealing with uncertainty.
  • Robust Generalization Tests: Introduces tasks requiring agents to reason effectively in scenarios explicitly outside standard physics (e.g., modified gravitational laws), enabling clearer measurement of agents' ability to generalize scientifically rather than memorizing common textbook results.
  • Direct Benchmarking against Expert Solutions: Provides expert (PhD-level astrophysics) reference solutions, enabling precise scoring and interpretability of agents' errors and strengths.

Benchmark Design and Methodology

We designed detailed gravitational two-body system simulations incorporating factors such as displacement of the center-of-mass, proper motion, drag forces, and modified gravitational laws (with power-law exponents differing from Newtonian gravity). Each of these distinctive scenarios tests not only raw scientific reasoning skill but also the strategies used by the model during experimentation and discovery.

Agents interact through an observation interface, capable of providing up to 100 data-points over a set simulation runtime. To succeed, agents must identify which measurements would most effectively reduce uncertainty, reflecting crucial scientific reasoning skills.

We define problem-specific accuracy thresholds based on comparison to expert solutions, modeling realistic experimental limitations rather than arbitrary correctness criteria.

Key Experimental Results and Insights

Our experiments with baseline agents‚Äîincluding state-of-the-art models GPT-4o and Claude 3.5‚Äîshow these systems consistently fail at careful experimental planning, significantly underusing their observation budgets. Overall performance dropped drastically when limited by observation budgets:

  • Under full-data access: The top model (o4-mini-high) achieved 74%, demonstrating moderate baseline capabilities in reasoning given abundant data.
  • Under limited observation budget: Performance dropped sharply, highlighting significant weaknesses in active information gathering and strategic reasoning under uncertainty.

Detailed qualitative analysis identified problematic shortcuts such as unjustified assumptions of symmetry and ungrounded physical constants (e.g. arbitrary mass assumptions). These systematic errors illustrate deeper misunderstandings of the iterative, uncertainty-driven nature of realistic scientific discovery.

A single demonstration of highly capable generalization was observed: o4-mini-high successfully reasoned about novel gravity deviations (modified exponent cases). Yet even this model showed no coherent strategy for planning observational budgets, underscoring systemic gaps there.

The trace below shows two illustrative observation-planning runs by Claude 3.5 Sonnet attempting to find the maximum stellar velocity with a 40-observation budget: one run systematically refines its estimate to achieve 2% error, while the other misinterprets velocity estimates from finer time resolution and incurs a 45% error due to poorly targeted subsequent observations.

Limitations, Reflections, and Future Work

This initial version of GravityBench focuses exclusively on gravitational two-body scenarios. While providing a controlled environment and initial rigorous testing, this restricts our benchmark to a narrow domain. Future expansions could incorporate:

  • Additional fields beyond gravitational physics (e.g., electromagnetism)
  • Realistic observational errors and constrained time-directional observations
  • Visual perception tasks for richer observational modalities

Despite limitations, GravityBench strongly illuminates critical weaknesses in leading AI agents' strategic reasoning and autonomous scientific discovery. We believe it represents foundational progress‚Äîrigorously framing these necessary capabilities, transparently quantifying gaps, and providing a platform to systematically address them.

GravityBench introduces rigorous, ground-truthed evaluations of AI scientific discovery skills, currently revealing clear deficiencies in iterative reasoning under uncertainty. We encourage the community's use and further development of GravityBench to gradually close these critical reasoning and strategy gaps, enabling meaningful progress toward autonomous scientific discovery in AI.
